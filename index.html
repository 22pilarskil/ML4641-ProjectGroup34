Introduction

        The stock market has long been analyzed in the attempt to gain an upper hand in the investing world. The earliest works in this field used LSTMs, models specializing in handling sequential data, to predict stock prices based on historical stock data (Vohra & Savaridassan). More advanced models recently have adapted, accounting for sentiment of corresponding news headlines in their predictions (Paul & Vishnoi). We will attempt to do the same using Miguel Aenlle’s stock headline dataset.

https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/miguelaenlle/massive-stock-news-analysis-db-for-nlpbacktests?resource=download

This dataset consists of 1.4 million entries, each composed of a headline, the stock ticker associated with the headline, and the date it was published. Pricing information is not immediately available, but can be obtained using Yahoo Finance’s API.

Problem

Our project aims to overcome limitations in existing models that struggle with the short-term volatility and randomness of stock prices. Current LSTM and RNN architectures which solely rely on historical stock data generalize poorly to unseen data due to the random walk nature of stock price changes (Selvin et. al). Market sentiment approaches often oversimplify by reducing complex headline data to a single sentiment score while ignoring market trends (Deveikyte et. al). Our approach will differ by integrating these two strategies into one, with financial metrics inputted along with textual data to provide a more robust and complete picture of the financial landscape in an attempt to balance the shortcomings of using each in isolation.  

Methods - Data Preprocessing

        Our data preprocessing has gone through several stages. We split the data preprocessing aspect of the project into two major tasks: preprocessing news headline data and then preprocessing financial metrics that correspond to these news headlines.

We initially considered using Miguel Aenlle’s stock headline dataset containing nearly 1.4 million entries. However, many headlines had unclear associations with specific stock tickers, prompting us to switch to a custom dataset. For example, a generic headline like “Stocks That Hit 52-Week Highs On Friday” was mapped to stock ticker “A” even though this headline did not explicitly mention the company Agilent (A). After facing difficulties with web scraping due to automation blocks, we utilized the Alpha Vantage API, which provided a more reliable source of news headlines for specific tickers. Our custom script pulled headlines from Alpha Vantage’s historical dataset, filtering to include only those explicitly mentioning the company or its ticker. This process resulted in a curated dataset of approximately 175,000 relevant headlines.

For the financial data, we gathered comprehensive metrics from each ticker’s IPO date using Yahoo Finance and calculated various secondary features based on these metrics. This included daily closing prices, volume, market cap, and calculated metrics such as logarithmic values, volatility, RSI, and moving averages. Our labels for the model were the surrounding changes in percent change of a headline – for example, if a headline pertaining to ticker A came out on August 12th, and the previous trading day was August 10th and the next trading day was August 13th, we would take the percent change from August 13th minus the percent change from August 10th as our labels. This data was compiled into a separate database, NumericalData. During runtime, we would look at the date associated with each headline in our dataset, and pull financial metrics from the two weeks leading up the headline as part of our input data package to the model.

        Our initial NumericalData database had two main problems. For one, there were repetitions of information, some examples being that Volume and Log Volume conveyed the same information and that MACD was a derivative of EMA 12 and EMA 26. Secondly, these metrics were unchecked: closes would drop to 0 and then increase again, resulting in an infinite percent change. Market caps were sometimes unavailable and reported as NaN values, while volatility was scaled by the close value and not the percent change, making it an outright poor measure. Not to mention that some tickers exhibited what we can only describe as suspicious pricing patterns – a day of high volume and low prices would be followed by a day of low volume and high prices, an increase of around several thousand percent yet a sharp decrease in activity.

        We refined our dataset, now termed NumericalData_refined, by streamlining and enhancing the financial metrics to address previous issues with data accuracy and relevance. We consolidated the features into essential metrics such as Percent Change, Log Volume, Log Market Cap, RSI, Volatility relative to Close, SMA Ratio, and SL_MACD to eliminate redundancies and focus on crucial data points. For example, we assessed volatility in proportion to the stock price to account for its significance relative to the stock's value, and compared SMA 10 and 20 only in relation to each other to detect trend deviations. We also handled NaN values by assigning a zero value to unknown market caps and disregarded any data where the stock price fell below $0.01 to avoid skewed percentage changes. Additionally, we removed entries exhibiting abnormal fluctuations on zero volume trading days, which typically resulted in drastic and unrealistic price changes. After removing stocks with persistent erratic behaviors—such as those with frequent changes exceeding 500%—we finalized a clean and reliable dataset, ready for analysis.

Methods - Machine Learning Techniques

In terms of machine learning techniques used, we began with an experiment to fine-tune Google’s BERT—a leading transformer model known for its prowess in natural language understanding. Our initial step involved adapting BERT to analyze stock market headlines, appending an output layer to categorize sentiment into three classes: positive, negative, and neutral. The promising results from this phase encouraged us further.

After this experiment, we integrated the financial metrics that were collected from Yahoo Finance by adding to our sentiment analysis model a customized transformer layer to process this numerical data. This was combined with the outputs from the textual sentiment analysis model from earlier through a dense layer, aiming to create a more comprehensive model. We explored both regression, to predict specific stock price changes, and classification approaches, to categorize price movements as up, down, or unchanged. Preliminary tests indicated that the regression model struggled with convergence, likely due to the high variance in stock price changes. Shifting focus to a classification model yielded improved outcomes, suggesting that categorizing price movement was a more effective strategy given the data’s inherent variability.

Results - Data Preprocessing

The collection of histograms, encompassing log volume, log market cap, RSI, percent change, MACD, Volatility/Close, and SMA Ratio, all exhibit transformational changes from their processed to their refined states, underscoring a systematic approach to financial data preprocessing. Initially, the processed data reveals certain commonalities across these features: a prevalence of extreme values, which typically indicates either a high occurrence of smaller entities (in the case of market cap and volume) or extreme market conditions (such as overbought/oversold scenarios depicted by RSI, sharp percent changes in stock prices, and significant divergences in MACD). Such distributions suggest a raw market landscape, rich with idiosyncrasies and marked by the pronounced impact of outliers.

Upon refinement, the datasets display a tempered distribution, where the extremes are noticeably reined in, indicating the application of data normalization techniques. This is particularly evident in the log volume and log market cap histograms, where the high initial peaks—suggestive of a multitude of low-volume trades and small market caps—give way to smoother gradients, reflecting a more equitably represented spectrum of market activity. For RSI, the reduction in tail heaviness and the smoothing of percent change histograms imply a mitigation of abnormal market conditions and price movements, leading to a depiction of the market that emphasizes typical trading conditions over anomalous events.

Lastly, the histograms for Volatility/Close and SMA Ratio further substantiate the trend of refinement. The processed data's focus on the more frequent, yet less impactful, ratios is broadened in the refined histograms to include a wider range of values, balancing the dataset. This shift not only reduces the bias towards lower volatility or more stable price movements relative to a moving average but also furnishes a clearer view into the nuanced dynamics of market behavior. Such refinement is key to ensuring that subsequent analytics, built on these metrics, can draw upon a foundation that is representative of the full market breadth, fostering robustness in analytical models and supporting more reliable financial decision-making.

Results - Model

Included below is a visualization of results obtained by our experimental sentiment model. We see that there is rapid convergence by the first epoch, but after continued training, the model overfits, as evidenced by a clear divergence between training and validation loss over the 10 epochs we trained for. If we were to use this model in practice, we would select the model weights obtained by epoch 1, as the validation loss has dropped significantly by that point and is still tightly correlated with the training loss.

Below are the training graphs for the three permutations of our classification model. In order to investigate the importance of financial metrics and headlines in computing class likelihoods, we trained two further models, one without the financial metrics transformer block and one without the headlines transformer block. As part of our preprocessing step, we took care to choose the thresholds for determining neutrality (changes within what +/- % that were minor enough to be considered “no movement”) to ensure that there was a balance of positive, negative and neutral labels. We ended up sticking with a threshold of 0.01, meaning any percent change less than 1% up or down would be a member of the neutral class, because it resulted in a near perfect split among the data (33% of data points belonging to each class).

Headlines and Metrics- Maximum test accuracy: 45.9%

Metrics only- Maximum test accuracy: 35.8%

Headlines only- Maximum test accuracy: 45.5%

An important finding when comparing the 3 models is that while they all converged rapidly, the model trained on just headlines performed significantly better than the model trained on just financial metrics. This is an interesting finding that supports the Weak-Form Efficient Market Hypothesis. Essentially, this hypothesis states that all prior information about a stock is already factored into the stock price, and as a result, technical analysis of a stock’s historical price data is fruitless. In other words, sudden and new information in the form of headlines is the main predictor of a stock price change.
